We're challenging the folklore of development, one myth at a time. Let's debug the narratives that shape our industry.

Unraveling the Fabric of Development

The Challenge

As the industry evolves, what was once accepted as truth can become a hindrance. Many "unwritten rules" and common pieces of advice, while seemingly logical, often lead to inefficiencies, missed deadlines, and unnecessary stress.

Our Mission

This is your guide to critically examining these beliefs. We'll expose the fallacies and help you distinguish between genuine best practices and outdated folklore, empowering you to make more informed decisions.

Read. Think. Click.

"Once we start coding, everything will fall into place."

→ Without clear requirements, confusion grows — not clarity.

"Users will figure out how it works."

→ Clear and intuitive UX should never be left to chance.

"Documentation is only for beginners."

→ Even experts forget details — documentation saves time and effort.

"You must be a math genius to code."

→ Most development is about problem-solving and logic — not advanced math. Basic algebra and logical thinking are enough for most dev roles.

"The best developers memorize everything."

→ Great devs know how to find answers, not memorize them. Google, docs, and Stack Overflow are your tools.

"Real programmers don’t use frameworks."

→ Frameworks save time and reduce bugs. Using them smartly is a sign of experience, not laziness.

"You must start coding young to succeed."

→ Many developers switch careers in their 30s, 40s, or later. Tech values skill, not age..

"You need a Computer Science degree to get hired."

→ Portfolios, projects, and practical skills often matter more than degrees — especially in web and frontend roles.

"Debugging means you're bad at coding."

→ Debugging is half of development. Even senior devs spend hours fixing bugs — it’s normal and expected.

"Developers don’t need soft skills."

→ Communication and empathy are essential for teams.

"Open source is just for fun."

→ It's a gateway to jobs, learning, and real-world impact.

"More features mean a better app.."

→ Too many features can hurt usability.
